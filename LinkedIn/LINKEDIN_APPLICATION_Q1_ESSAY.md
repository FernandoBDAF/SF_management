# LinkedIn Backend Engineering Apprenticeship - Question 1: Your Personal Story and Impact

> **Question:** Tell us how your unique experiences and background shape the point of view you will bring to LinkedIn. Tell us how you have demonstrated continuous perseverance and tenacity to achieve a long-term goal or overcome challenges.
> **Word limit:** 400-700 words

---

I grew up and started my career in Brazil, and my path into software engineering was not traditional, but it was very intentional. I spent many years in science (I reached PhD studies in Bioinorganic Chemistry) and later I became an entrepreneur, building my company from zero to a stable business over ten years. In both environments, I learned to respect complexity and also to be disciplined about evidence: you don't guess your way out of a lab result, and you also don't guess your way out of financial operations. This background shapes the way I approach engineering problems today: I care a lot about correctness, traceability, and building systems that people can trust.

Because I worked in science, business, and now software, I usually look at problems from multiple angles. I think about the user impact, the operational risks, and the long-term maintainability — not only "does it work now." For example, in my recent backend projects, I focused on audit trails, authentication/authorization, performance tuning, and observability, because I learned the hard way that reliability is not optional. I also bring a perspective of someone who had to communicate across different teams and roles (technical and non-technical), and in a second language. My English is good but not perfect, so I naturally value clarity, simple explanations, and good documentation. I think this matches LinkedIn's culture of collaboration and building systems that are used by many different people at massive scale.

Another part of my perspective is that I genuinely enjoy learning, and I'm not afraid to start from "I don't know yet." I committed to building a real CS foundation on my own — not to collect titles, but because I like understanding how things work under the hood. I applied that learning immediately to real projects. This is one reason I'm excited about the apprenticeship: I want mentorship and a strong engineering environment where learning is part of the job, and where I can apply that learning to production systems.

About perseverance and tenacity: my whole career is basically a long exercise of that. Doing PhD-level research requires patience, dealing with failed experiments, and still showing up every day. Later, building a company for ten years required even more persistence — solving problems with limited resources, making decisions under uncertainty, and taking responsibility when something breaks. I also built SF Management (more on this in question 3), an internal system for financial operations and reconciliation, because scaling the business demanded it and there was no one else to do it. That taught me ownership and the habit of finishing what I start.

More recently, I showed this same perseverance in my transition to software engineering. I was already running a stable business, but I had discovered I genuinely enjoyed building software — not just managing it. Moving to the US gave me the opportunity to pursue this seriously. I studied consistently, built projects, and took a junior SWE role at a startup to gain hands-on experience. I shipped production code, learned from feedback and mistakes, and took on tasks like migrating authentication systems, improving observability, and supporting cloud deployments. When something goes wrong in production, you don't quit; you debug, you learn, you fix it, and you improve the system so it doesn't happen again.

To summarize: my unique background (science rigor, entrepreneurship ownership, cross-cultural communication) shapes how I approach engineering — I care deeply about correctness, clarity, and building systems people can trust. And my perseverance is proven: I've pushed through failed experiments, built a company over ten years, and committed fully to a career transition that required starting over. I'm ready to bring both to LinkedIn.
