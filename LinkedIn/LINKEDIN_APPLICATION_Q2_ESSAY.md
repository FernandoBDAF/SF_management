# LinkedIn Backend Engineering Apprenticeship - Question 2: Your Journey Into Engineering

> **Question:** What was the origin of your interest in programming? How have you grown your skills? Why is the Backend Engineer role the right next step for you?
> **Word limit:** 500-700 words

---

## 1) Spark

My interest in programming started early, even before I understood it could be a real career. As a teenager, I played online games and got curious about how things worked—trying small code to customize tools. It was not "serious engineering," but it gave me that first feeling: with code you can change reality.

Later, in college, I worked with scripts related to DNA databases (data handling and automation), and I really liked it. When I entered university I was divided between Chemistry and Computer Science. I chose chemistry and went deep into it (eventually doing PhD studies). But the programming seed never went away — years later, when my business hit scaling problems, I remembered how much I enjoyed making systems work.

What surprised me is how much the developer mindset now permeates other parts of my life. I started treating recurring behaviors like debugging: looking for root causes and feedback loops instead of just trying harder. I observe patterns, form a hypothesis, change the system, then iterate. Even my communication improved — I now write clearer "requirements" in real life (scope, assumptions, acceptance criteria) and sometimes use AI to document decisions. The result is less friction and more predictable outcomes.

## 2) Growth

My real growth happened when programming stopped being only curiosity and became a tool to solve painful real-world problems. In my company, as we grew, spreadsheets started to break. We were doing more transactions, more reconciliation, and the risk of mistakes increased a lot. So I hired a software company to build our first internal system — an MVC app that I co-built with them while learning to code.

I started as owner, translating business needs into requirements. But I became more involved — reviewing code, taking a bootcamp at MITxPRO, completing dozens of courses. Over time, I wasn't only "requesting features" — I was understanding design choices, spotting issues, and eventually taking over solo. About two years ago, I refactored it into a modern REST API (SF Management) that I still maintain.

**Beyond bootcamps:** I made a deliberate choice to build a real CS foundation. I watched MIT lectures on YouTube, and I've read parts of about 15 CS and software engineering books — not to collect titles, but because I truly believe a strong foundation allows everything else to grow sustainably. I learned that from my academic experience: results are not obvious at the beginning, but the depth pays off. The books that had the most impact were on clean code, design principles, architecture, and system design. I'm currently reading Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective, which goes much deeper into how computers actually work.

To get professional experience, I took a junior SWE role at a startup called SuaQuadra. There I shipped production code in Go, supported deployments with AWS and Kubernetes, and worked on authentication migration (Keycloak to Auth0) — which taught me how to change critical infrastructure without breaking users.

---

## 3) Role Fit

I'm specifically interested in the Backend Engineering Apprenticeship because it fills a critical gap in my growth: mentorship. I've learned a lot independently, but I want to learn from experienced engineers, tackle real large-scale challenges, and see how world-class teams operate. That's something you can't get from books alone.

**What I bring:** I'm confident in my ability to manage AI coding assistants effectively, translate business rules into clean code, and improve system design iteratively. My SF Management project demonstrates these skills (detailed in my Q3 response).

**What I want to learn:** I want to go deeper on system design at scale, distributed systems, and eventually work with AI/ML features in production. LinkedIn is the right place: massive data, high traffic, reliability requirements, and systems that evolve without downtime.

I chose backend specifically because it feels closest to my science background — you're working with the fundamentals of computer science, solving problems that require deep technical knowledge. Backend is where correctness, performance, and reliability really matter. My long-term goal is to combine deep backend expertise with my business experience to eventually grow into engineering leadership. I believe my path — science discipline, entrepreneurship ownership, and production engineering experience — makes me a strong fit for this apprenticeship.
